## Learning Goal

Understand the difference between a Minimum Viable Test (MVT) and a Minimum Viable Product (MVP), and learn how to identify and test the riskiest assumptions behind any product idea before committing to building.

## Review Notes

These are for after you read the article, or if you come back to this page again later.

### Core Insight

A Minimum Viable Product (MVP) is often too expensive and too late.

Before building anything, aim for a **Minimum Viable Test (MVT)**: the smallest, cheapest experiment that reduces uncertainty about your biggest assumption.

The goal isn't to validate your idea. The goal is to **invalidate your assumptions** as quickly and cheaply as possible.

---

### MVT vs MVP

| Attribute | Minimum Viable Test | Minimum Viable Product |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Purpose | Designed to learn | Designed to deliver value |
| Engineering | May not require building any software | Requires engineering effort |
| Scope | Tests a single assumption | Bundles many assumptions together |
| Cost | Fast, cheap, disposable | More expensive and slower |
| Success | Success = learning | Success = users repeatedly receiving value |

Never build an MVP until the biggest assumptions have already survived several MVTs.

---

### Every Product Idea Is Just a Stack of Assumptions

Examples:

- Do people actually have this problem?
- Is the problem painful enough to solve?
- Will they change their current behaviour?
- Will they pay?
- Will they trust us?
- Can we acquire customers economically?
- Can we technically deliver the solution?

Your job isn't to defend these assumptions — it's to attack them.

---

### Test the Riskiest Assumption First

Not every unknown deserves equal attention.

Identify the assumption that would **kill the business** if it were false.

Examples:

- Nobody wants this.
- Nobody will pay.
- Customers don't trust automation.
- Users won't switch from their existing workflow.

Test this assumption before anything else.

---

### Build Experiments, Not Features

Instead of asking:

> "What should we build?"

Ask:

> "What do we need to learn?"

Possible experiments:

- Landing pages
- Fake door tests
- Concierge MVPs
- Wizard of Oz experiences
- Customer interviews
- Manual fulfilment
- Smoke tests
- Ads measuring interest
- Email signups
- Pre-orders

The experiment should be the smallest thing capable of answering one question.

---

### Learning Velocity Is a Competitive Advantage

Good product teams don't win because they build faster.

They win because they learn faster.

Every experiment should reduce uncertainty.

If nothing was learned, the experiment failed.

---

### Kill Bad Ideas Early

Discovering an idea won't work is progress.

A failed MVT can save months of engineering effort and thousands of dollars.

Celebrate invalidated assumptions — they prevent expensive mistakes.

---

### Don't Fall in Love with Solutions

Customers don't care about your idea.

They care about solving their problem.

Stay attached to the problem, not your proposed solution.

---

### Practical Framework

For every new idea, ask:

1. What assumptions does this idea depend on?
2. Which assumption is the riskiest?
3. What's the cheapest experiment that could test it?
4. What result would change my mind?
5. Run the test.
6. Learn.
7. Repeat.

---

### PM Takeaway

One of the most valuable skills as a Product Manager is not deciding *what to build*.

It's deciding **what not to build yet** until enough uncertainty has been removed.

Great PMs maximise learning before they maximise shipping.
