# Prioritization in Product Management

One of the biggest surprises people have when they move into product management is that **prioritization is rarely about choosing the "best" idea**. It's about choosing the best idea for this organization, at this moment, with these constraints.

---


## The Challenge of Prioritization

Imagine you've just joined a startup. Within your first week, people start sending you ideas:

- Marketing wants referral codes.
- Sales wants an enterprise dashboard.
- Engineering wants to rewrite the authentication system.
- Customer Support wants a better notification center.
- The CEO wants AI.

Every one of these requests has a reasonable argument behind it. Every team can explain why their request is urgent. If you simply asked everyone to vote, you'd end up with five "top priorities." But the reality is that a product team can only do one or two meaningful things at a time.

This is why prioritization exists.

> **Prioritization isn't the art of saying "yes" to the best ideas. It's the discipline of saying "not now" to good ideas so that the organization can focus on the few that matter most.**

---

## Beyond Frameworks: Understanding Organizational Values

Many new product managers think prioritization starts with frameworks like **RICE, MoSCoW, ICE, or Value vs. Effort**. Those frameworks are useful, but they're calculators, not compasses. Before you score anything, you need to understand what the organization actually values.

The criteria you've listed represent five different lenses through which every initiative should be examined. Let's walk through each of them.

---

## 1. Organizational Impact

> _"If we build this, how does it move the company forward?"_

This question is intentionally broader than asking whether users will like a feature. Companies don't exist merely to ship features; they exist to achieve strategic goals such as:

- Entering a new market
- Increasing profitability
- Becoming the market leader in a category
- Improving customer retention
- Building capabilities that support future products

**Example:**  
Spotify might consider a karaoke mode. It sounds fun and users might enjoy it. But if Spotify's current strategic objective is to dominate podcasts and audiobooks, karaoke may contribute very little toward that mission. Meanwhile, improving audiobook recommendations could directly support the company's long-term direction.

The better feature isn't necessarily the more exciting one. It's the one that advances the organization's strategy.

> Every feature should have a line connecting it to a strategic objective. If you can't draw that line, the feature probably isn't a priority.

---

## 2. Business Value Added

Product decisions must create economic value. Business value doesn't always mean immediate revenue; it can also mean:

- Reducing costs
- Preventing customer churn
- Making sales easier
- Avoiding expensive operational problems

**Example:**  
Amazon's one-click purchasing reduced friction during checkout, leading to enormous business value. Conversely, a feature allowing users to customize the checkout button color may have minimal business value unless it significantly changes behavior.

Product managers evaluate business value by asking:

- Will this increase revenue?
- Will this reduce operating costs?
- Will customers stay longer?
- Will it improve pricing power?
- Will it create a competitive advantage?

> Every engineering hour is an investment and should generate a return.

---

## 3. User Reach and User Success

Product management exists because businesses serve customers. A feature that generates revenue but doesn't create value for users eventually stops generating revenue.

- **User Reach:** How many people benefit?
- **User Success:** How much do they benefit?

**Example:**  
Fixing a typo seen by five million users vs. reducing checkout time by 30 seconds for 10,000 paying customers. The typo affects more people but has a tiny impact; the checkout improvement affects fewer people but meaningfully improves their experience.

Good PMs measure both reach and intensity.

> Products should solve real problems and focus on outcomes, not implementations.

---

## 4. Technical Feasibility

Understanding how features are built is crucial. Technical feasibility involves appreciating engineering realities such as:

- Technical debt
- Localization challenges
- Payment systems
- Tax compliance
- Infrastructure scaling
- Regulatory requirements

Great PMs ask engineers:

- What assumptions are we making?
- What could go wrong?
- How certain are these estimates?
- What technical debt are we creating?
- Can we simplify?

Sometimes technically "boring" work produces the greatest long-term velocity.

> Every roadmap should reflect what is realistically achievable with available resources.

---

## 5. Time-Based Risk

Timing affects a feature's value. Missing critical windows can be costly.

**Example:**  
Integrating with a payment provider before holiday shopping vs. redesigning the settings page. Missing the holiday season may cost millions, while postponing settings redesign has little consequence.

Great PMs ask:

- What happens if we don't build this now?
- Will competitors gain an advantage?
- Will regulations change?
- Will customer expectations shift?
- Is there a narrow market window?

> Timing itself can be part of the value proposition.

---

## Outcome Importance

This criterion ties everything together. Organizations use **Objectives and Key Results (OKRs)**:

- **Objectives:** Where the organization wants to go.
- **Key Results:** How success will be measured.

A product initiative should contribute directly to one or more outcomes.

> Outputs vs. Outcomes:
>
> - Output: "We shipped dark mode."
> - Outcome: "User retention increased by 8%."

Outcome importance forces every feature to answer:

> _"What measurable change are we trying to create?"_

If unclear, the feature probably isn't ready.

---

## Putting It All Together

Imagine evaluating a feature for scheduling recurring donations:

- Supports organizational strategy? Yes.
- Creates business value? Probably.
- User reach? Potentially every recurring donor.
- Technical feasibility? Seems feasible.
- Urgency? Before a fundraising campaign.
- Contributes to OKRs? Yes.

Compare to adding animated badges:

- May increase engagement slightly.
- Contributes less to strategy and revenue.
- Uncertain impact on donor behavior.

Both are good ideas, but only one is the right priority.

---

## Final Thoughts

Prioritization is a series of informed trade-offs. Different organizations weigh criteria differently depending on their stage:

- Early-stage startups prioritize user reach and learning.
- Growth-stage companies emphasize revenue and retention.
- Mature enterprises focus on reliability, efficiency, and strategic alignment.

There is no universal formula.

The product manager's role is to understand the organization's context and guide conversations to invest limited time where it creates the greatest impact.

> Experienced PMs ask:  
> _"Given everything we're trying to achieve right now, is this the most valuable thing we could be working on?"_

This shift transforms prioritization from feature ranking into strategic decision-making.
