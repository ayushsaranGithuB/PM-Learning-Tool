# What Is Product Management?

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the core job of a product manager
- Distinguish outcomes from outputs
- See why PM work sits between business, design, and engineering

## Why This Matters

Product management is one of those jobs that looks vague from the outside. Engineers write code. Designers shape interfaces. Marketers run campaigns. A PM can seem like the person in the middle who schedules meetings and moves tickets around.

That picture is incomplete.

The real reason product management exists is that teams need someone thinking clearly about what problem matters, why it matters now, and how the team will know whether solving it actually worked.

## Core Idea

A product manager reduces uncertainty so a team can build the right thing with the right people at the right time.

That does not mean the PM has all the answers. It means the PM helps the team answer the most important questions:

- What problem are we solving?
- Who has this problem?
- Why is it worth solving now?
- What would success look like?
- What trade-offs are we making?

## Mental Model

One useful way to think about PM work is this:

Product management is not feature management. It is decision management.

Features are outputs. Decisions determine whether those outputs create outcomes.

## Example

Imagine a team at a music app notices that many users sign up but never return after the first day. One response is to brainstorm features: playlists, badges, social sharing, recommendations.

A stronger PM response starts earlier:

- Where exactly are users dropping off?
- What did they expect to happen?
- Which users are leaving?
- Is the problem activation, value, or habit?

The PM's job is to help the team frame the problem before the team rushes into shipping.

## Builder Notes

For engineers, this shift matters. It is easy to assume the hard part is building the feature well. Often the harder part is confirming the feature is worth building at all.

For designers, the shift is similar. A polished flow can still fail if it solves the wrong problem or serves the wrong users.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating the roadmap like a list of promises instead of a learning plan
- Confusing shipped work with successful work
- Thinking the PM owns every decision alone

## Reflection

- Think about a product you use often. What problem does it solve well?
- Think about a feature that felt unnecessary. What question do you think the team failed to answer first?
