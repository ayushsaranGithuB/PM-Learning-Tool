## Learning Goal

Understand why Product Management exists, what Product Managers actually do, and why the role is less about having all the answers and more about asking the right questions.

---

## Introduction

From the outside, Product Management can seem like one of the vaguest jobs in technology.

Engineers write code.

Designers create interfaces.

Marketers acquire customers.

So what exactly does a Product Manager do?

Many people assume the PM is responsible for writing requirements, managing the backlog, or running meetings.

Those are responsibilities, but they are not the purpose of the role.

The real purpose of Product Management is to help teams make better decisions.

A Product Manager reduces uncertainty by helping a team understand which problems are worth solving, why they matter, and how success will be measured.

> **Product Management isn't about having all the answers. It's about learning to ask better questions.**

That idea will become a recurring theme throughout this notebook.

---

## Why Product Management Exists

Building software is expensive.

Every feature requires engineering effort, design work, testing, documentation, support, and ongoing maintenance.

The biggest risk is rarely building something badly.

It's building the wrong thing.

Product Management exists to reduce that risk.

Before committing weeks or months of work, someone needs to ask:

- Is this a real problem?
- Who experiences it?
- How important is it?
- Why should we solve it now?
- How will we know we've succeeded?

Great Product Managers help teams answer these questions before solutions are proposed.

---

## Asking Better Questions

A common mistake is jumping straight to solutions.

"We need dark mode."

"We need AI."

"We need a mobile app."

Experienced Product Managers slow the conversation down.

Instead they ask:

- What problem are we trying to solve?
- What evidence do we have?
- Who is affected?
- How are users solving this today?
- What assumptions are we making?
- What trade-offs are involved?

These questions help teams avoid building features that nobody needs.

---

## The PM at the Intersection

No single discipline can answer every important product question.

Business asks:

> Is this commercially viable?

Engineering asks:

> Can we build this reliably?

Design asks:

> Will people actually want to use this?

A Product Manager works at the intersection of all three.

Their role is to balance competing priorities, facilitate difficult decisions, and align the team around solving the right problem.

This relationship is often described using three overlapping perspectives:

- **Desirability** — Does this solve a real customer problem?
- **Feasibility** — Can we build and maintain it?
- **Viability** — Does it make business sense?

Great products exist where these three perspectives overlap.

## The Product Manager's Mindset

One useful way to think about Product Management is this:

> Product Management is not feature management. It is decision management.

Features are outputs.

Good decisions create outcomes.

A Product Manager's job is to help the team make informed decisions with the best information available, while accepting that uncertainty can never be completely eliminated.

## Key Takeaways

- Product Management exists to reduce uncertainty.
- Great PMs ask better questions before proposing solutions.
- The role sits at the intersection of Business, Design, and Engineering.
- Product Managers are responsible for outcomes, not simply shipping features.
- Success comes from understanding problems before building solutions.

## Reflection
