# Metrics and Growth

## Learning Goal

Understand how Product Managers use analytics to understand user behavior, prioritize work, and make better product decisions.

A successful product team doesn't measure everything. It identifies the few metrics that best represent whether users are finding value and uses those metrics to guide experiments and product improvements.

This chapter introduces the foundations of product analytics and the mindset behind growth.

---

# What this chapter covers

After completing this chapter you should understand:

- Why analytics is essential for Product Managers
- The difference between vanity metrics and actionable metrics
- The Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Monetization framework
- How teams choose a primary metric to optimize
- Why growth is often removing friction rather than spending more on marketing
- How experimentation drives continuous product improvement

# Why analytics matters

Imagine standing behind every user while they use your product.

You'd immediately notice:

- where they get confused
- what they ignore
- what they love
- where they leave

Obviously that's impossible.

Analytics is the next best thing.

Every click, search, signup, purchase, cancellation and return tells part of the story of how people experience your product.

A Product Manager's job is to learn from those signals.

Without analytics you're guessing.

With analytics you can observe behavior at scale.

---

# Products are always imperfect

One of YC's recurring observations is that nearly every product is rough when real users first start using it.

No matter how much user testing or QA happens before launch, customers will inevitably use the product in unexpected ways.

This means:

- users discover edge cases
- workflows break
- assumptions prove false
- unexpected behaviours emerge

Analytics helps identify these problems long before someone reports them.

Instead of asking:

"Why are users unhappy?"

You can often ask:

"Where exactly are users leaving?"

---

# The Growth Funnel

Most internet products can be viewed as a simple funnel.

```
Acquisition
      ↓
Activation
      ↓
Retention
      ↓
Monetization
```

Each stage asks a different question.

## Acquisition

Can we get people to discover the product?

Example metrics:

- New users this week
- Organic signups
- Referral traffic
- Conversion from landing page

---

## Activation

Do users experience value quickly?

Activation is usually the most important leading indicator.

Examples:

- Completed onboarding
- Created first project
- Uploaded first file
- Sent first message

Many successful products discover that users who complete one key action are dramatically more likely to become long-term customers.

Finding that action is one of Product Management's most valuable jobs.

---

## Retention

Do people come back?

Retention is often considered one of the strongest indicators of product-market fit.

Rather than looking at total users, Product Managers follow cohorts.

Example:

100 users signed up this week.

After:

- Week 1 → 60 remain
- Week 2 → 48 remain
- Week 4 → 35 remain

This tells you far more than total downloads ever could.

Retention measures habits.

---

## Monetization

Does the product create sustainable value?

Examples include:

- New revenue this week
- Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)
- Average Revenue Per User (ARPU)
- Subscription conversion

Revenue is important.

But for early products, improving activation and retention often leads to revenue naturally.

---

# Leading vs Lagging Metrics

Not every metric is equally useful.

## Lagging Metrics

These tell you what already happened.

Examples:

- Revenue
- Total users
- Downloads
- Lifetime revenue

They are important, but difficult to influence immediately.

---

## Leading Metrics

Leading metrics predict future success.

Examples:

- Users completing onboarding
- Weekly Active Users
- Messages sent
- First purchase
- Projects created

Improving leading indicators usually improves lagging metrics later.

---

# Vanity Metrics

Some metrics look impressive but provide very little insight.

Examples:

- Total downloads
- Registered accounts
- Page views
- Social media followers

These numbers often increase while the product itself isn't improving.

Good metrics explain behavior.

Vanity metrics decorate dashboards.

---

# Metrics drive teams

Analytics isn't only for Product Managers.

As companies grow, engineering, marketing and product teams all need measurable goals.

Instead of saying:

> "Let's grow."

A team might define a concrete objective:

Current:

218 organic signups per week

Goal:

300 organic signups per week by the end of the month.

Projects:

- Improve SEO
- Launch referral program
- Simplify landing page
- Publish educational content

Every day the team watches the metric.

Did it improve?

If not, why?

This creates a culture of measurable progress rather than subjective opinions.

---

# Growth is experimentation

Growth isn't simply advertising.

Much of product growth comes from removing friction.

A Product Manager continuously asks:

- Where do users leave?
- Why do they leave?
- What assumption failed?

Every improvement follows the same cycle.

```
Hypothesis
      ↓
Experiment
      ↓
Measure
      ↓
Learn
      ↓
Repeat
```

Great product teams don't expect every experiment to succeed.

They expect every experiment to teach them something.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Analytics helps Product Managers observe user behaviour at scale.
- Products are always imperfect after launch.
- Measure behaviours rather than surface-level numbers.
- Focus on leading indicators before lagging outcomes.
- Retention usually matters more than downloads.
- Growth is primarily about reducing friction.
- Every improvement should be measurable.
- Data should inform decisions, not simply populate dashboards.
